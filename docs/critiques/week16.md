# Week 16 Self-Critique

**Scope of this critique.** Week 16 ran two notebooks and one headless script across three end-to-end ES configurations on top of Prime Intellect's Wordle-specialized Qwen3 checkpoints: (A) `notebooks/week16_wordle_es_qwen_sft.ipynb` ŌĆö the format/parser probe at `enable_thinking=False`, `MAX_NEW_TOKENS=64` that uncovered the 100% parse-failure mode and motivated the production thinking-on configuration; (B) `notebooks/week16_wordle_es_qwen.ipynb` and `scripts/run_week16_es.py` ŌĆö the headline `enable_thinking=True`, `MAX_NEW_TOKENS=512` configuration, run three times: ES on `ŌĆ”-Wordle-SFT` with raw gradients, ES on `ŌĆ”-Wordle-RL` with raw gradients, and ES on `ŌĆ”-Wordle-RL` with normalized gradients. The headline result is that **all three production runs produced `final eval_success = 0.0%` and `best eval_success = 12.5%` at an early iteration that is statistically indistinguishable from cold-base eval noise**, and that the parse-failure-rate diagnostic (`fb%`) sat at 100% across all three runs across every iteration. The critique below is about why this happened, what it does and does not entail about ES on Wordle-tuned LMs, and why we are calling this the project's terminal experiment.

## ORIENT

### Strengths

- **The pivot itself was the right call.** Weeks 10/12/14 spent five weeks training Gemma and then Qwen3 base models from scratch with warm-start + ES + LoRA, and the only positive result we ever produced was Week 12 Session 8's `+22pp` at `VOCAB_SCHEDULE=[16]`, which never generalized. Week 16 stops fighting that particular curve and asks the next obvious question ŌĆö "does ES add anything on top of an already-Wordle-trained checkpoint?" ŌĆö using the same `train_es_wordle` machinery so the answer is comparable across weeks. The negative answer is informative; spending another week on yet another generic-LM warm-start would not have been.
- **The format/parser probe (notebook ┬¦2.5) is exactly the kind of cheap experiment we missed in Week 14.** Notebook A (`week16_wordle_es_qwen_sft.ipynb`) ran `enable_thinking=False`, `MAX_NEW_TOKENS=64` first specifically to surface failure modes inside an hour rather than overnight. It produced 100% parse failure and the diagnostic was unambiguous (the model exhausted its 64-token budget inside `<think>...</think>` and never emitted `<guess>`). Flipping thinking on and giving 512 tokens of generation budget made the runs scoreable. This is the "qwen_probe profile" Week 14's critique said we needed; it worked.
- **`fb%` repurposed as parse-failure rate is load-bearing.** When the action representation changed from trie-masked char generation to `model.generate` + `<guess>` parsing, the `fb%` column in the verbose log was repurposed to report `parse_failures / parse_attempts`. That column is the single most important number in the Week 16 console logs: 100% across every iteration of every run is what tells us the layered fallback parser is doing all the work, not the LM. Without it we would be reporting "ES did nothing" without being able to distinguish "ES did nothing because the LM didn't change" from "ES did nothing because the LM did change but our parser couldn't tell."
- **`NORMALIZE_GRADIENT` as a third run is a clean ablation.** Run 2 (raw RL) had `ŌĆ¢─ØŌĆ¢` bouncing across two orders of magnitude (200 ŌåÆ 19,400) within a single training run, which is a known failure mode for ES on small populations: a single noisy-but-large gradient produces a single huge step (Run 2's iter-2 `|╬ö| = 2.61` against the calibration target of 0.13) that the next iteration cannot undo. Run 3 normalizes gradients and the per-step `|╬ö|` is exactly 0.13 every iteration. The fact that Run 2 and Run 3 produced essentially identical eval trajectories says the ALPHA / step-size axis is **not** the binding constraint at this base/budget. That is a real finding; previous weeks had multiple critiques pointing at step-size as the next thing to tune, and we can now say with evidence that it is not.
- **Best-iter restore is wired up and used correctly.** All three runs end with `RESTORE_BEST_ON_FINISH=True` reloading the iter-1 (or iter-8) checkpoint, and the FINAL ATTRIBUTION block reports `exported adapter %` separately from `last checkpoint %` so the reader can distinguish the optimization trajectory from the checkpoint we actually wrote to disk. This was originally a Week 12 Session 8 plumbing change; Week 16 is the first place it has been load-bearing for the headline number.
- **Artifact discipline is strong.** Each of the three runs persists `console.log`, `lora_adapter/` (PEFT-format, reloadable), `es_history.pkl`, and `plots/training_curves.png` under `runs/week16_es/{sft_base, rl_base, rl_base_normed_gradients}/`. Anyone reproducing the week needs only the three console logs to recover the headline numbers and the three adapter directories to reload the trained checkpoints.

### Areas for Improvement

- **`fb%` = 100% is the actual headline finding and we initially under-weighted it.** A persistent 100% parse-failure rate means the Wordle-tuned Qwen3 checkpoints, even with `enable_thinking=True` and 512 tokens of generation budget, never produce a clean `<guess>WORD</guess>` that the regex can extract ŌĆö every guess goes through the layered fallback (strip `<think>` blocks ŌåÆ first 5-letter alphabetic run ŌåÆ `XXXXX` sentinel). That fact alone is enough to explain why ES could not measurably improve any of the three runs: the LM-controlled "guess word" signal is mostly being chosen by `_FALLBACK_SKIP` heuristics in `src/wordle_qwen_policy.py:_parse_word_from_text`, not by the LM's distribution. ES cannot improve a policy whose guess is determined by string-postprocessing, not by the model. The Week 16 report mentions this in passing; it should have been the abstract.
- **We did not measure cold-base performance with the same parser.** The notebook ┬¦2.5 cold-base eval prints `SFT_cold Ōēł 6%` and `RL_cold Ōēł 12%`, but those numbers were collected with the *same* layered fallback parser applied in `WordleQwenPolicy`. So the comparison "ES did 12.5% / cold did 12%" is really "the parser produces ~12% on the RL base regardless of whether LoRA is attached." We do not have a clean number for "cold base + the canonical Prime Intellect rollout harness," which is what would let us say "ES added 0pp" instead of "ES added 0pp ┬▒ parser-noise."
- **`N_POP=8` is two orders of magnitude below what ES typically wants.** Salimans et al. (2017) used `N_POP` in the thousands; we ran 8 because each ES iteration requires `(2┬ĘN_POP+1)┬ĘN_EVAL_EPISODES┬ĘMAX_TURNS` `model.generate` calls at `MAX_NEW_TOKENS=512`, and `N_POP=64` would have made each iteration take ~hours on the available A100/H100 nodes. The honest read of three flat runs at `N_POP=8` is "we did not give ES enough population to estimate a credible gradient on a 1.6M-dim LoRA"; it is **not** "ES does not work on Wordle-tuned LMs." Failing to draw that distinction in the Week 16 report would overclaim.
- **`N_ITER=15` is short for ES on a fresh-LoRA initialization.** Even with normalized gradients, `ŌĆ¢╬ĖŌłÆ╬ĖŌéĆŌĆ¢` only reached 0.50 over 15 iterations. The Week 12 Session 8 PASS-A result on Gemma needed 60 iterations and `ŌĆ¢╬ĖŌłÆ╬ĖŌéĆŌĆ¢ Ōēł 4.9` to land its `+22pp` lift; the Week 16 runs are a quarter of that horizon and at most a tenth of the total displacement. We chose 15 because each iteration is so expensive on Qwen3, but choosing it does not make 15 sufficient. A negative result at `N_ITER=15` does not entail a negative result at `N_ITER=100`.
- **The `enable_thinking=True` decision left a confound on the table.** With thinking on, `MAX_NEW_TOKENS=512` is consumed mostly by the `<think>` block, and the `<guess>` tag often arrives near the end of the budget ŌĆö when it arrives at all. With thinking off, the parse-failure rate was 100% in the ┬¦2.5 probe; with thinking on, the parse-failure rate is *also* 100% according to the verbose log. That means the parser is failing for two different reasons in the two regimes (truncation vs format drift), and we did not separately quantify the `<guess>` parse-failure rate vs the "first-5-letter-run fallback was a real word" rate. A single extra counter in `_parse_word_from_text` (clean-`<guess>` matches vs fallback-real-word matches vs `XXXXX`-sentinel matches) would have made the diagnostic actionable; right now `fb%` collapses three failure modes into one.
- **`per_iter_secret_subset_size=8` against an `EVAL_N_EPISODES=16` greedy slate is the same lottery-ticket regime Week 12 critiqued.** The Week 12 Session 8 finding was that mini-batch CRN with rotating 4-secret subsets needed step-size shrinkage to converge. Week 16 runs subsets of 8 secrets per iteration but evaluates on 16 secrets greedy ŌĆö *and* the cold-base win rate is so low (~6-12%) that even with 8 secrets per training subset, most population members win 0 episodes per secret per iteration. Several of the high-fitness training spikes (Run 2 iter 4: `Fit=2.86`, `ES_win=26.6%`) followed by 0% greedy eval are textbook "the population found a training-set lottery ticket, not a transferable improvement." The Session 8 fix (quarter ALPHA + double iters) was specifically for this failure mode and we did not apply it.
- **No multi-seed runs.** Each of the three runs is a single seed (`SEED=42`). Given the per-iter eval noise floor (`Žā Ōēł 8.3pp` at one episode per secret on a 16-secret slate), a single-seed `best = 12.5%` vs cold `Ōēł 6-12%` is uncalibrated as an effect size. Two more seeds at the same configuration would have cost the same compute as Run 3 each and would have answered the most basic falsification question ("is the 12.5% peak a property of the optimizer or of the random seed").
- **The "ES contribution: n/a" line in the FINAL ATTRIBUTION block is honest but unsatisfying.** The block prints `RL_ceiling: n/a`, `SFT_cold: n/a`, `ES contribution: n/a` because the cold-base eval and the ES eval use different RNG streams. The fix is one extra eval call at the start of training under the same RNG stream as the post-training eval; we did not implement it. As written, the final attribution has to be reconstructed by reading the ┬¦2.5 cold-base eval out of the notebook output and subtracting it manually, which is exactly the kind of friction the FINAL ATTRIBUTION block exists to remove.
- **The headline failure may be a property of Prime Intellect's checkpoints, not of ES.** `ŌĆ”-Wordle-RL` is a checkpoint that has *already been RL-trained on Wordle*. Asking ES with a tiny LoRA and an 8-member population to add to that is asking a much weaker optimizer to improve on a much stronger one's local maximum. Week 16 cannot distinguish "ES is a bad optimizer for this task" from "ES is a fine optimizer but the basin around `ŌĆ”-Wordle-RL` is too narrow for the LoRA + small-population perturbation kernel to escape." We need the SFT-base run (which we have) plus a similar run on the *generic* Qwen3 base but with everything else identical (which we do not ŌĆö Week 14 used different action representation, prompt, and budget) to even start to disambiguate.

### Critical Risks / Assumptions

- **Risk: the parser is the entire experiment.** If `_parse_word_from_text` is the thing actually choosing 80%+ of the guesses (because `<guess>` extraction fails and the fallback hits a `_FALLBACK_SKIP`-passing 5-letter run from the model's reasoning text), then ES is not training a Wordle policy at all ŌĆö it is perturbing a base LM whose post-perturbation outputs feed a deterministic string-selector, and the selector's behavior is dominated by which English 5-letter words happen to appear in arbitrary reasoning traces. This is a different failure than "ES doesn't work on Qwen3"; it is "the experimental harness is not measuring what it claims to."
- **Risk: at `N_POP=8`, every Week 16 negative is consistent with `N_POP=8` being insufficient.** The honest conclusion is "ES at our budget did not measurably improve a Wordle-tuned Qwen3 base." The unsupported expansion would be "ES cannot improve a Wordle-tuned LM," and the report should not invite that reading. The presentation needs to be careful about which of the two it claims.
- **Risk: the `fb%` repurposing makes the verbose log column ambiguous across weeks.** In Weeks 12/14 `fb%` is the trie fallback rate (a property of char-mode); in Week 16 it is the `<guess>` parse-failure rate (a property of the generation parser). The same column number means different things in different weeks; anyone reading the histories without the week-specific notebook context will draw wrong conclusions. The fix is either a column rename (`parse_fail%`) when `action_granularity="char"` *and* the policy is `WordleQwenPolicy`, or per-policy verbose-log schemas. Neither was done.
- **Risk: `RESTORE_BEST_ON_FINISH=True` with no multi-seed eval encourages the report to overclaim the "best eval_success" number.** The 12.5% best in Run 1 / Run 2 / Run 3 is **2 wins out of 16 eval episodes**; under a Bernoulli null at the cold-base rate it is well inside the 95% confidence interval. The report says this; the postscript needs to keep saying it loudly because it is the easiest number to misread off the FINAL ATTRIBUTION block.
- **Assumption: `_FALLBACK_SKIP` is a complete enough denylist for the parser fallback path.** It contains 30-some 5-letter English words that are common in reasoning text. If Qwen3's reasoning vocabulary contains 5-letter words *not* in the list (and the explicit `PLAYI` truncation entry suggests the list was already padded for tokenizer artifacts), the fallback will sometimes return one of them as the "guess" and the env will count it as a real attempt. We do not have a count of how often this happens; it is part of the same `fb%` aggregate.
- **Assumption: bf16 base + fp32 LoRA gives a clean enough gradient surface for ES.** This was a deliberate change from Week 14's all-bf16 setup, motivated by pre-flight noise observations. We did not ablate it (i.e., we did not run the same Week 16 config with `cast_lora_to_fp32=False` to confirm it would have been worse). The ablation would cost one extra run and is the cheapest "did this knob matter" experiment we still owe.

## DECIDE

### Concrete Next Actions

The pre-registered "concrete next actions" template in previous critiques assumed there was a next experiment in the same direction worth running. Week 16 is the point at which that stops being true; the next actions below are about closing out the project cleanly, not about another optimization sweep.

1. **Treat Week 16 as the project's terminal experiment on "ES + LoRA on Wordle-tuned Qwen3."** The presentation will report Weeks 14 and 16 together as the negative result that closes the line of inquiry started in Week 10. Spending another week running `N_POP=64` on the same setup is unlikely to flip the answer at our compute budget, and even if it did, the result would not change the project's headline conclusion (which is about the operating window of small-population ES on top of Wordle-specialized base models, not about whether some bigger ES configuration can squeeze out a `+1pp` improvement).
2. **Backfill a proper cold-base eval under the same parser and the same RNG stream.** This is the one missing number that would let us replace `ES contribution: n/a` with a real subtraction. ~30 min of compute on the existing notebook by adding a single `quick_eval_success` call before ES starts under the post-ALPHA-probe RNG snapshot.
3. **Disaggregate `fb%` into three counters in `_parse_word_from_text`** ŌĆö clean-`<guess>` matches, fallback-real-word matches, `XXXXX` matches ŌĆö and rerun a single ES-on-RL iteration to get a clean read on which failure mode is actually 100%. ~1 hour of code + compute. This is the diagnostic that should have been built before the production runs.
4. **Run a single-seed sanity check at `N_POP=32`, `N_ITER=30`, `enable_thinking=True`, `MAX_NEW_TOKENS=512`, `ŌĆ”-Wordle-RL` base** ŌĆö the configuration closest to "what Salimans et al. would have called the minimum viable budget" that still fits inside the available wall-clock. If this also produces flat eval, the negative result is robust; if it produces a measurable lift, the Week 16 report's "we did not give ES enough budget" caveat needs to be elevated to the abstract. Time-box at one A100 day.
5. **Document `_FALLBACK_SKIP` and the parse-failure pipeline as a separate methodology note.** If a reader comes back to this work in six months, the single most important piece of context is "the LM does not produce parseable outputs in the harness we used; the fallback parser does the work." That belongs in `src/wordle_qwen_policy.py`'s module docstring (it is partially there) *and* in `docs/development_log.md` so it survives independently of any one notebook.
6. **Stop iterating on ES variants for this submission.** No PGPE-lite revisit, no LoRA rank sweep on the Wordle-tuned base, no second character-mode attempt with a redesigned mask. We have run enough variants across enough weeks; further estimator/representation tuning is not the bottleneck and would not change the presentation.

## Postscript: Reading Weeks 10-16 together

The strong reading of this critique is that the project's animating thesis ŌĆö "warm-start a generic LM on Wordle, then let ES specialize the LoRA" ŌĆö is true inside a narrow operating window (Week 12 Session 8 PASS-A at `VOCAB_SCHEDULE=[16]`, mini-batch CRN, quartered ALPHA, Gemma + word-softmax) that we have repeatedly failed to leave. Each post-Week-12 attempt has changed something the operating window depended on:

- **Week 14** changed the base LM (Gemma ŌåÆ Qwen3) and the action representation (word-softmax ŌåÆ trie-masked char generation) at the same time. Net result: no signal at vocab=16, no signal at vocab=32, run killed.
- **Week 16** changed the warm-start (ours ŌåÆ Prime Intellect's SFT/RL), the action representation (trie-masked char ŌåÆ `model.generate` + `<guess>` parser), the prompt format (chat template + thinking blocks), and the ES horizon (`N_ITER=100/stage` ŌåÆ `N_ITER=15`). Net result: three runs, all converged to `final eval_success = 0.0%`, `best eval_success = 12.5%` at iter 1 or 8 ŌĆö uncalibrated from cold-base eval noise.

What the Week 16 negative result rules in: the project's small-population ES + LoRA pipeline does not measurably improve a Wordle-tuned Qwen3 checkpoint at our budget. What it does not rule in: that ES + LoRA on a Wordle-tuned LM cannot work at any larger budget, that the choice of `MAX_NEW_TOKENS`/`enable_thinking` was optimal, or that the parse-failure-rate diagnostic is fully understood. Those are the caveats the presentation needs to land cleanly.
